So, Who am I?

I’m your friendly neighbourhood vision scientist and optometrist. I spend my days geeking out how our eyes and brain team up to make sense of the world. My work dances between neuroscience, vision science, and a touch of tech wizardry, tackling everything from how we process the world to why our two eyes don’t always play nice.

Whether I’m decoding how the brain processes vision, designing software, or sneaking video game mechanics into diagnostic tools (because why not?), my goal is simple: to make eye care smarter, more accessible, and a lot less boring.

Selected Research Project

A comparison of Random-Dot E stereoacuity test and SART - Pilot Study

The study compared the performance of the Random-Dot E (RSTE) stereoacuity test, a traditional card-based method, with the Stereoacuity Assessment and Research Tool (SART), a novel digital tool I developed. It aimed to evaluate their agreement and differences in measuring stereoacuity thresholds, providing insights into the potential of SART as a more accessible and innovative alternative for clinical and research applications.

Current Project

Epidemiology of Glaucoma and its Treatment in Africa: A Systematic Review

This systematic review aims to address the significant public health burden of glaucoma in Africa, where primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG) is the leading cause of irreversible blindness due to late presentation, low awareness, and poor access to healthcare. By analyzing the epidemiology, risk factors, and treatment options for glaucoma, the study seeks to bridge knowledge gaps and inform public health strategies for early detection and improved management, particularly in resource-limited settings.

Publications

Interocular Symmetry of Retinal Nerve Fiber Layer and Optic Nerve Head Parameters Using Optical Coherence Tomography in a Ghanaian Population.

This study establishes normative data on interocular symmetry of retinal nerve fiber layer (RNFL) thickness and optic nerve head (ONH) parameters in a Ghanaian population, finding that average RNFL thickness in the right eye is slightly greater than in the left, while most ONH parameters show no significant interocular difference. These findings provide a critical reference for glaucoma detection and monitoring in Ghana, where glaucoma prevalence is among the highest worldwide.
